### Let's quickly play with some numbers to reacquaint ourselves with the basic syntax we need.
Enter the commands listed below into your <strong>developer tools JavaScript console.</strong>
<p>1. First of all, let's declare a couple of <strong>variables</strong> and initialize them with an <strong>integer</strong> and a <strong>
float</strong>, respectively, then type the <strong>variable names</strong> back in to check that everything is in order:

<p>const myInt = 5;<br>
const myFloat = 6.667;<br>
myInt;<br>
myFloat;</p>

<p>2. <strong>Number values</strong> are typed in without quote marks — try declaring and initializing a couple more <strong>variables</strong> containing <strong>numbers</strong> before you move on.

<p>3. Now let's check that both our <strong>original variables</strong> are of the <strong>same datatype.</strong> There is an operator called <strong>typeof</strong> in <strong><em>Javascript</em></strong> that does this. Enter the below two lines as shown:

<p>typeof myInt;<br>
typeof myFloat;</p>

You should get <em>"number"</em> **returned** in both cases — **this makes things a lot easier** for us than <em>if different numbers had different data types</em>, and we had to deal with them in different ways. Phew!
